# Iran War Oil Crisis — 4-Week Impact Simulation

## Situation Summary (as of March 20, 2026)

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched joint military strikes on Iran, killing Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei. Iran retaliated by effectively closing the Strait of Hormuz — a chokepoint through which 20% of the world's daily oil supply and 19% of global LNG trade normally flows. Tanker traffic through the strait has dropped from 150+ daily transits to near zero.

Brent crude surged from $65-70/barrel pre-war to $120/barrel within three weeks. Analysts at Wood Mackenzie warn $150 is likely and $200 is "not outside the realms of possibility." On March 19, Iran struck a Qatar LNG facility, destroying 17% of Qatar's export capacity. The US has threatened to "take over" the Strait of Hormuz militarily.

OPEC+ members led by Saudi Arabia and Russia agreed to add 206,000 barrels/day in April — far short of the 4+ million barrels/day disrupted by the Hormuz closure. Global oil markets face the largest supply disruption since the 1973 oil crisis.

## Key Actors and Positions

### Energy Market Decision-Makers

**Prince Abdulaziz bin Salman, Saudi Energy Minister** — Controls OPEC+ output decisions. Saudi Arabia has 2-3 million barrels/day of spare capacity but is cautious about flooding the market — higher prices benefit Saudi revenues. Faces pressure from the US to increase production. Must balance revenue maximization against the risk of demand destruction and recession that would ultimately hurt Saudi interests.

**Alexander Novak, Russian Deputy PM (Energy)** — Co-leads OPEC+ alongside Saudi Arabia. Russia benefits from high oil prices but its own exports are partially sanctioned and redirected to China/India at discounts. Russia has limited incentive to increase production and is quietly pleased that Western economies face energy pain.

**Amos Hochstein, US Senior Energy Advisor** — Biden-era holdover now advising the Trump administration on energy security. Pushing Saudi Arabia and UAE to increase output. Coordinating potential Strategic Petroleum Reserve releases. Caught between wanting lower prices for US consumers and the administration's "maximum pressure" Iran policy that caused the crisis.

### Importing Nations Under Pressure

**Hardeep Singh Puri, India's Petroleum Minister** — India imports 90% of its crude oil, with roughly half transiting Hormuz. Every $10/barrel increase adds $17-18 billion to India's annual import bill. Indian refiners had been buying discounted Russian crude, but war-risk insurance premiums have surged 400%, erasing the discount. The Indian rupee is under pressure. India's GDP growth forecasts are being cut by multiple banks.

**Ken Saito, Japan's Economy Minister** — Japan sources 90% of its crude from the Middle East, with 75% transiting Hormuz. Japan has the highest risk score (6.4) among all nations for Hormuz disruption. Northeast Asian LNG prices have doubled to $22.5/MMBtu. Japan holds 4.4 million tons in LNG reserves — roughly 2-4 weeks of supply. Japanese refiners have requested government stockpile releases.

**Chris Bowen, Australia's Energy Minister** — Australia imports 90% of its liquid fuel. Petrol prices have jumped 50 cents/litre (from $1.69 to $2.19) since the war began. Australia holds only 36 days of petrol reserves, 34 days of diesel, and 32 days of jet fuel — and has been non-compliant with IEA emergency reserve requirements since 2012. Bloomberg reports even major energy exporter Australia is vulnerable because it exports raw resources but imports refined fuel.

### Market Analysts and Traders

**Jeff Currie, Former Goldman Sachs Head of Commodities** — Veteran oil market voice. Has been warning that structural underinvestment in oil production since 2020 means there is no spare capacity to absorb a Hormuz-scale disruption. Argues $150+ is inevitable if the strait remains closed through Q2.

**Fatih Birol, IEA Executive Director** — Leads the International Energy Agency coordinating emergency oil stockpile releases among member nations. Has called for coordinated SPR releases but member nations are divided on timing and volume. The IEA holds about 1.2 billion barrels in emergency reserves across member states — roughly 30 days of disrupted Hormuz flows.

### Geopolitical Actors

**IRGC Naval Commander, Rear Admiral Alireza Tangsiri** — Commands Iran's Strait of Hormuz operations. Iran has deployed mines, fast attack boats, and anti-ship missiles to enforce the closure. Iran's stated position: the strait remains closed until all attacks on Iranian territory cease and sanctions are lifted. Iran has demonstrated willingness to strike Gulf state energy infrastructure (Qatar LNG attack on March 19).

**@EnergyTwitter (composite: energy analysts, traders, journalists on X)** — The public conversation. Tracking real-time tanker movements via MarineTraffic, analyzing satellite imagery of Iranian naval deployments, debating price forecasts, and amplifying any leak or rumor. This account represents the collective voice of energy market social media — bullish, fearful, and data-driven.

## Simulation Parameters

### Prediction Question
If the Iran war continues for the next 4 weeks (through April 17, 2026) with the Strait of Hormuz remaining effectively closed, what happens to:
1. Global oil prices (Brent crude trajectory)
2. Fuel prices and economic impact in Australia, India, and Japan
3. OPEC+ production response and its adequacy
4. Likelihood of coordinated IEA strategic reserve release
5. Risk of the conflict escalating to direct strikes on Saudi/UAE oil infrastructure
6. Consumer and political backlash in importing nations

### Simulation Focus
Model the interactions between energy policymakers, market traders, and geopolitical actors as they respond to a prolonged Hormuz closure. Each agent should act according to their national interests, institutional constraints, and the evolving market data.
